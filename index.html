    Time traveller's recap:

    In the beginning, there is Mosaic. Or at least this is the beginning for most of the public. It's 1990, three years before Mosiac's official launch, the Internet is largely private to research facilities & the military, it looks different for every method of access, & there is only 'WorldWideWeb' (WWW). Or at least as far as our future ideas of browsers are concerned. Built by CERN - the nuclear research agency behind the Large Hadron Collider of the future - WWW is officially released to the public.

    What's all the fuss? Well, imagine leaping from command lines & specialized, inconsistent text tools, to an all-in-one solution utilizing the simple power of WYSIWYG.

    Behold the glory:

    It's 1991 now, & HTML itself is only mentioned on the Intenet by one of the CERN bigwigs, then floats about in the ether while other developments are made. The most notable is an early version of WWW called 'ViolaWWW'. It sees it's release in 1992 as the first browser to utilize scripting, stylesheets, & tables. Fun fact: as of November of 1992, there are a total of 26 websites in existence. ViolaWWW is only built only for the X Window System (no relation to Windows or Microsoft), which leads to it's quick demise.

    Welcome to April of 1993! WWW's souce code has been released to the pubic & has been updated under the new name 'Nexus'. It's now capable of basic stylesheets (thanks Viola!), downloading & opening files with supported MIME types (movies, sounds, etc.), browsing newstroups, spellchecking, using protocols such as FTP & HTTP, & can even display inline images! Despite all this, it quickly falls beneath the shadow of Mosaic.

    Mosaic, Cello, & Netscape

    It's still April of 1993, & the public - knowing next to nothing of WorldWideWeb - is ravenous for a browser, but they don't know it yet. Mosaic is released & the world whets their palates, that is, everyone not using Windows 3.1, which runs on PCs that aren't powerful enough to run Mosaic.

    Windows users don't have to wait long as Cello is released in June. Originally created for lawyers - by lawyers - Cello was lauded for its simple interface, relatively low system requirements, & didn't require a TCP/IP stack to install. But in November Mosaic is released for Windows, & Cello falls into the archive of "historical browsers".

    Cello in action:

    As we enter 1994, we watch Mosaic create a massive spike in the number of Internet users. The layman can now access the Interwebz from their home or office PCs, which were also becoming more mainstream literally by the month. Given we know the future, we may hesitate to call Mosaic mainstream; rather a launching pad for what's to come.

    What makes Mosaic especially tasty? Two (2) main things:

    1. It's so easy to start - difficulty launching apps is apparently a thing - even doctors can do it.
    2. Pictures. Images were displayed alongside text as they would be in magazines. Wasn't really a thing before now.

    Late in the year, Mosaic rebrands to Netscape & hits the ground running with Netscape Navigator. It boasts custom fonts, background colors, & embedded media. It's cross-platform (Apple Macintosh, Windows 3.1, Linux, Solaris, et al.), promises encryption & server authentication, offers proprietary online services, & even raises your children for you. By 1995, less than 2 years after it's beta release, Netscape goes public & earns an IPO value of $2.9 billion in U.S. currency, & has successfully created its own market. They were so successful that engineers from Microsoft visited Netscape's offices to "get a lay of the land" before they assess their own plans for breaking into a clearly lucrative market.

    I WILL BLEED YOU & ERASE YOU FROM EXISTENCE

    It's 1995 & the TCP/IP soup is beginning to simmer. Netscape has 80% of the market share thanks to Navigator, & is rolling out silly things like HTML frames, image maps, cookies, & even the first iteration of Javascript. It's so popular "Net-scaping" is used interchangably with "surfing the web".

    Netscape Navigator 2.0 for Mac (1995):

    Then there's Microsoft, aggresively run by Bill Gates, who's been dutifully ignoring the Internet to this point & thus have no footing in a growing market overlapping his own. Once Netscape released their IPO, Gates reacts with an about-face after his engineers visit with Netscape. Microsoft has officially claimed the meeting was simply "a laidback exchange of ideas". Netscape accounts pretty much the opposite, going on-record to say Microsoft offered them a one-time $1,000,000 for unlimited access to their products & codebase, or would face retaliation.

    Netscape lawyers up; Microsoft bullies up, restructuring the vision & roadmap of Microsoft to be extricably connected to the Internet using Netscape's business model & superior resources. Months later, Internet Explorer (IE) is born.

    Perhaps unironically, v1 code of IE is licensed from Spyglass Mosaic, an original fork of Netscape's early work. IE v1 sucks. Hard. IE v2 is released in October, still lacking features, but leagues improved over its predecessor with changes like HTML table support, SSL, cookies, VRML, & newsgroups.

    BROWSER

    On December 7th of 1995, Bill Gates unironically holds a press conference saying "I'mma kilt Netscape dead," or something to that effect. Unfortunately, he's done it before, so to WWWar we go!

    FIGHT!
    • 1995
      • Microsoft visits/chills/threats Netscape
      • Microsoft announces Internet Explorer
      • Netscape releases update (v1.22) & IPO; makes boo-koos.
        • 15 days later, Microsoft releases IE v1.
      • Netscape continues to wipe the floor with IE.
      • Microsoft releases IE v2, boosted by sales of Windows 95.
      • Microsoft wages war on all competition on the 7th so they can be king of jungle.
    • 1996
      • Netscape & Microsoft release new updates months apart, adding features & plugins, effectively creating bloatware & slowing down their products, but who cares! It's war!
      • Opera is launched, but one one cares.
      • Netscape ramps up is features in v3 with spiffy wizardry like tables, frames, form autofilling, & advanced Javascript.
      • Netscape officially peaks with a browsershare of 90%.
      • Microsoft adopts Active X, frames, & reverse-engineers Javascript with "JScript". So original!
      • IE v3 is released for FREE bundled with Windows 95.
      • CSS is released with limited implementation for several years.
        • IE welcomes it, surprisingly
        • Netscape is slow to adopt it, giving us developer blue balls
        • Users get their first tastes of unrendered banners with text like "Best displayed on Netscape", or "Best displayed on Internet Explorer". Everyone loves it.
    • 1997
      • Netscape & IE both release v4.
      • Market shares begin to change as Windows grows in popularity with the PC market, & thus IE by (free) extension.
      • Netscape holds 72% of the market; Microsoft has 18%.
      • Microsoft dumps crazy money into distributing & pushing IE.
    • 1998
      • Netscape goes open-source, now free for all.
      • Microsoft captures 50% of the market.
      • AOL purchases Netscape for $4.2 billion.
      • Microsoft goes on trial for antitrust violations.
      • BONUS: CSS2 is released, & developers finally take note.
    • 1999
      • Microsoft captures just shy of 80% of the market.
      • Microsoft's antitrust case continues
    • 2000
      • Microsoft's loses its antitrust case, but is eventually overturned by an appeals court.
      • Bill Gates finds some humility.
    • 2001
      • The war's officially won by Microsoft when Netscape is discontinued, though support via AOL continues through 2008.

    STANDARIZATION

    These are the boring years, quite frankly. The Internet opens up to more of the world, culminating in 1,372,354,708 people online worldwide. Google watches, waits, & positions itself for the future. Internet Explorer plateaus & declines.

    Other stuff:

    • Mozilla 1.0 is release, but goes largely unnoticed.
    • Internet Explorer continues to dominate, reaching peak market share of 92% in 2004, & discontinues support for Macintosh.
    • Apple offers Safari to the world in 2003. The world's unimpressed until 2005 when Safari receives an overhaul, & because Apple's sole endorsed & shipped browser.
    • With Navigator long dead, Mozilla launches Firefox as the world's premiere open-source browser, gaining trackion quickly with those disillusioned with Microsoft, &/or seeking better privacy & security.
    • Opera does some weird things as trialware, becoming ad-sponsored, then ditching it all after financial support from Google (foreshadowing) in 2005.
    • Opera finds its stride in 2006 with fraud protection & enhanced security features
    • W3C & other organizations work to develop web standards

    GOING MOBILE & THE LAUNCH OF CHROME

    It's 2007, & Steve Jobs is excited. After 3 years of development, iPhone is released, & with it Safari 3 for both OSX & iOS. It's even ported to Windows for some reason. While Palm Pilots & Windows Mobile phones have been on the fringe of the consumer sphere for several years, none have the impace of iPhone with it's big, sexy 3.75" screen & multi-touch gestures. Browsers on these smartphone precursors were typically built into the operating system.

    Motorola, Samsung, & Asus aren't far behind, all sporting their own native browsers. But who cares? 2008's here!

    Enter GOOGLE

    Rmember when they were just a search engine that wasn't Yahoo!? September of 2008 is a turning point for Google, & arguably the direction of how we use the internet. While Apple continues to revel in the success of iPhone, iPhone, iChargeTooMuch, etc., Google makes this September one to remember. (Really hard not to make a Guy Fawkes joke here.)
