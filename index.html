    Time traveller's recap:

    In the beginning, there is Mosaic. Or at least this is the beginning for most of the public. It's 1990, three years before Mosiac's official launch, the Internet is largely private to research facilities & the military, it looks different for every method of access, & there is only 'WorldWideWeb' (WWW). Or at least as far as our future ideas of browsers are concerned. Built by CERN - the nuclear research agency behind the Large Hadron Collider of the future - WWW is officially released to the public.

    What's all the fuss? Well, imagine leaping from command lines & specialized, inconsistent text tools, to an all-in-one solution utilizing the simple power of WYSIWYG.

    Behold the glory:

    It's 1991 now, & HTML itself is only mentioned on the Intenet by one of the CERN bigwigs, then floats about in the ether while other developments are made. The most notable is an early version of WWW called 'ViolaWWW'. It sees its release in 1992 as the first browser to utilize scripting, stylesheets, & tables. Fun fact: as of November of 1992, there are a total of 26 websites in existence. Viola is only built only for the X Window System (no relation to Windows or Microsoft), which leads to its quick demise.

    Welcome to April of 1993! WWW's souce code has been released to the pubic & has been updated under the new name 'Nexus'. It's now capable of basic stylesheets (thanks Viola!), downloading & opening files with supported MIME types (movies, sounds, etc.), browsing newstroups, spellchecking, using protocols such as FTP & HTTP, & can even display inline images! Despite all this, it quickly falls beneath the shadow of Mosaic.

    Mosaic, Cello, & Netscape

    It's still April of 1993, & the public - knowing next to nothing of WorldWideWeb - is ravenous for a browser, but they don't know it yet. Mosaic is released & the world whets their palates, that is, everyone not using Windows 3.1, which runs on PCs that aren't powerful enough to run Mosaic.

    Windows users don't have to wait long as Cello is released in June. Originally created for lawyers - by lawyers - Cello was lauded for its simple interface, relatively low system requirements, & didn't require a TCP/IP stack to install. But in November Mosaic is released for Windows, & Cello falls into the archive of "historical browsers".

    Cello in action:

    As we enter 1994, we watch Mosaic create a massive spike in the number of Internet users. The layman can now access the Interwebz from their home or office PCs, which were also becoming more mainstream literally by the month. Given we know the future, we may hesitate to call Mosaic mainstream; rather a launching pad for what's to come.

    What makes Mosaic especially tasty? Two (2) main things:

    1. It's so easy to start - difficulty launching apps is apparently a thing - even doctors can do it.
    2. Pictures. Images were displayed alongside text as they would be in magazines. Wasn't really a thing before now.

    Late in the year, Mosaic rebrands to Netscape & hits the ground running with Netscape Navigator. It boasts custom fonts, background colors, & embedded media. It's cross-platform (Apple Macintosh, Windows 3.1, Linux, Solaris, et al.), promises encryption & server authentication, offers proprietary online services, & even raises your children for you. By 1995, less than 2 years after its beta release, Netscape goes public, earns an IPO value of $2.9 billion in U.S. currency, & has successfully created its own market. They're so successful that engineers from Microsoft visit Netscape's offices to "get a lay of the land" before they assess their own plans for breaking into a clearly lucrative market.

    I WILL BLEED YOU & ERASE YOU FROM EXISTENCE

    It's 1995 & the TCP/IP soup is beginning to simmer. Netscape has 80% of the market share thanks to Navigator, & is rolling out silly things like HTML frames, image maps, cookies, & even the first iteration of Javascript. It's so popular "Net-scaping" is used interchangably with "surfing the web".

    Netscape Navigator 2.0 for Mac (1995):

    Then there's Microsoft. Aggresively run by Bill Gates, they've been dutifully ignoring the Internet to this point, & thus have no footing in a growing market overlapping its own. Once Netscape releases their IPO, Gates reacts with an about-face after his engineers visit with Netscape. Microsoft has officially claimed the meeting was simply "a laidback exchange of ideas". Netscape suggests pretty much the opposite, going on-record to say Microsoft offered them a one-time $1,000,000 for unlimited access to their products & codebase, or face retaliation.

    Netscape lawyers up; Microsoft bullies up, restructuring the vision & roadmap of Microsoft to be extricably connected to the Internet using Netscape's business model & superior resources. Months later, Internet Explorer (IE) is born.

    Perhaps unironically, v1 code of IE is licensed from Spyglass Mosaic, an original fork of Netscape's early work. IE v1 sucks. Hard. IE v2 is released in October, still lacking features, but leagues improved over its predecessor with changes like HTML table support, SSL, cookies, VRML, & newsgroups.

    BROWSER

    On December 7th of 1995, Bill Gates unironically holds a press conference saying "I'mma kilt Netscape dead," or something to that effect. Unfortunately, he's done it before, so to WWWar we go!

    FIGHT!
    • 1995
      • Microsoft visits/chills/threats Netscape
      • Microsoft announces Internet Explorer
      • Netscape releases update (v1.22) & IPO; makes boo-koos.
        • 15 days later, Microsoft releases IE v1.
      • Netscape continues to wipe the floor with IE.
      • Microsoft releases IE v2, boosted by sales of Windows 95.
      • Microsoft wages war on all competition so they can be king of jungle.
    • 1996
      • Netscape & Microsoft release new updates months apart, adding features & plugins, effectively creating bloatware & slowing down their products, but who cares! It's war!
      • Opera is launched, but one one cares.
      • Netscape ramps up is features in v3 with spiffy wizardry like tables, frames, form autofilling, & advanced Javascript.
      • Netscape officially peaks with a browsershare of 90%.
      • Microsoft adopts Active X, frames, & reverse-engineers Javascript with "JScript". So original!
      • IE v3 is released for FREE bundled with Windows 95.
      • CSS1 is released with limited implementation for several years.
        • IE welcomes it, surprisingly.
        • Netscape is slow to adopt it, giving us developer blue balls.
        • Users get their first tastes of unrendered banners with text like "Best displayed on Netscape", or "Best displayed on Internet Explorer". Everyone loves it.
    • 1997
      • Netscape & IE both release v4.
      • Market shares begin to change as Windows grows in popularity with the PC market, & thus IE by (free) extension.
      • Netscape holds 72% of the market; Microsoft has 18%.
      • Microsoft dumps crazy money into distributing & pushing IE.
    • 1998
      • Netscape goes open-source, now free for all.
      • Microsoft captures 50% of the market.
      • AOL purchases Netscape for $4.2 billion.
      • Microsoft goes on trial for antitrust violations.
      • BONUS: CSS2 is released, & developers finally take note.
    • 1999
      • Microsoft captures just shy of 80% of the market.
      • Microsoft's antitrust case continues.
    • 2000
      • Microsoft loses its antitrust case, but is eventually overturned by an appeals court, setting some unfortuante prescendents for the future Big Tech & government oversight.
      • Bill Gates finds some humility.
    • 2001
      • The war's officially won by Microsoft when Netscape is discontinued, though support via AOL continues through 2008.

    STANDARIZATION

    These are the boring years, quite frankly. The Internet opens up to more of the world, culminating in 1,372,354,708 people online worldwide. Google watches, waits, & positions itself for the future, releasing two updates to its search algorithm in the interim. Internet Explorer nears its plateau.

    Other stuff:

    • Mozilla 1.0 is released, but goes largely unnoticed.
    • Maxthon is released in Singapore. The whole of China will catch on one day.
    • Several specialized browsers, such as Flock, SeaMonkey, & a host of others - mostly based on Mozilla forks - rise & fall with little notice outside the accessibility & social networking communities.
    • Internet Explorer continues to dominate, reaching peak market share of 92% in 2004, & discontinues support for Macintosh.
    • Apple offers Safari to the world in 2003. The world's unimpressed until 2005 when Safari receives an overhaul, & becomes Apple's solely endorsed & shipped browser.
    • With Netscape avigator long dead, Mozilla launches Firefox as the world's premiere open-source browser, gaining trackion quickly with those disillusioned with Microsoft, &/or seeking better privacy & security.
    • Opera does some weird things as trialware, becoming ad-sponsored, then ditches it all after gaining financial support from Google (foreshadowing) in 2005.
    • Opera finds its stride in 2006 with fraud protection & enhanced security features, especially with Opera 9.
    • W3C & other organizations work to develop web standards.

    GOING MOBILE & THE LAUNCH OF CHROME

    It's 2007, & Steve Jobs is excited. After three years of development, iPhone is released, & with it Safari 3 for both macOS & iOS. It's even ported to Windows for some reason. While Palm Pilots & Windows Mobile phones have been on the fringe of the consumer sphere for several years, none have the impact of iPhone with its big, sexy 3.75" screen & multi-touch gestures. Browsers on these smartphone precursors were typically built into the operating system.

    The iPhone functions as a catalyst into the next era the Internet, the way we use it, & ultimately how we develop for it. Apps haven't so much a thing before now. Nor have media queries. But plasma screens & wide monitors are already pushing the necessity for CSS to adapt. There are a LOT of messy sites during this time. Most banking &/or govenment sites require IE to use despite Firefox already being established as a more secure alternative. IE itself, however, hasn't really changed much since 1998.

    Motorola, Nokia, Samsung, & Asus aren't far behind in the smartphone race, all sporting their own native browsers. But who cares? 2008's here!

    Enter GOOGLE

    Rmember when they were just a search engine that wasn't Yahoo!? September of 2008 is a turning point for Google, & arguably the direction of how we use the Internet. While Apple continues to revel in the success of iPhone, iPod, iChargeTooMuch, etc., Google makes this September one to remember. (Really hard not to make a Guy Fawkes joke here.)

    To create Chrome, Google poaches several devs from Firefox to build atop Apple's rendering engine, WebKit, in order to support apps like Google Maps there are "always-on". Chrome is also the first successful browser to utilize tabs as we know them today, though Opera has had them for years. I'm still unsure if tabs help with my ADD, or exacerbate it.

    1. Google announces the Chromium Projects as completely open-source, immediately gaining eyeballs & excitement from that community, as well as peer review & constant bettering of the tools.
    2. They drop a public beta of Chrome on the 2nd. It's strictly for XP, but has people's attention because Google's still a respected company at this point. Chrome v1.0 is released in December with a macOS/Linux version dropping summer of 2009.
    3. On September 28th, they drop Android on the HTC Dream. Just look at this sexy beast:
    4. Chrome had 30 million users just 9 months after launch due largely in part to its reputation for speed. I'm sure that will always be the case.

    It's worth mentioning 2008 is also when HTML5 & CSS3 hit the public as it affects certain browser trajectories. At any rate, it's been too long since we've had a war, right? Let's fix that.

    THE COLD (BROWSER) WAR

    Another browser war? Sort of. I've found several articles describing it as such. It reads like more of a Cold War at best.

    Based on our age group I have to assume most of us are trudging through college at this point in time, or worse, figuring out what the hell to do with shiny new adult selves post-graduation. Some of us are already on our 2nd & 3rd career trajectories. As such, paying attention to what's happening with web browsers from 2008-2022 is likely not high on anyone's list.

    Unlinke the actual Cold War this one's pretty boring.

    2008-2015
    • 2008
      • Microsoft releases Windows 7; the world remains butthurt & clings to XP; IE is still the Browser Daddy.
      • Yahoo! rejects Microsoft's proposed acquisition at $31 per share. I'm sure they won't regret it.
      • Google launches all out war on other web-focused companies.
      • Netscape Navigator officially retires to the annals of time with v9 being the last.
    • 2009
      • Google launches Chrome OS in preparation for the coming Chromebook invasion, & releases Chrome's extenstion gallery open to third party integration.
      • Firefox overtakes IE 7 with a 32% market share - which will prove to be its peak - though not all versions of IE as a whole.
      • Opera continues to plug away with an update every year, pleasing existing Opera users, but failing to garnish new ones.
      • A curious, minimalist browser called Pale Moon is forked from Firefox & the world fails to notice, even 14 years later.
    • 2010
      • There are more than 8,500 extension, & 1,500 themes available for Chrome's now 120 million users.
      • Chrome captures 15% of the market share in just over a year.
      • Google launches the Web Store.
    • 2011
      • Chromebooks are released, running the first "browser OS", Chrome OS.
      • Chrome is released on Android.
      • Microsoft releases IE 9 with a "clean" new design. Cool.
      • Firefox releases "Nightly" (alpha build) & "Developer Edition" (beta build) versions of the browser.
    • 2012
      • Google releases Chrome for iOS.
      • Chrome topples IE with 31% market share. 👑
      • Microsoft release IE 10 that tries to integrate with Windows 8's weirdness.
    • 2013-2015
      • In 2013, Microsoft releases IE 11, the final version.
      • Two years later, Microsoft announces how much they miss browser money, & unveil Edge.
      • Opera switches from its Presto engine to Chromium to stay atop speed & development trends, successfully dividing its community. Turns out people already dislike Google.
    2016-2022
    • Chrome

      Chrome continues to expand across platforms & dominate the market. They take the lead in setting prescedence on things like making HTTP pages insecure & limiting their access (2018), adding the ability to run Linux apps, integrating a fairly solid password saving system, & adding auto-play to its UX. Google effectively builds upon its already massive tech empire with things like Google Home & Wi-fi, & undertakes the years-long process of unifying its moving parts into single accounts with controllable, tiered access.

    • Safari

      Safari continues to evolve & gain traction simply by being the default Mac browser, though remains a bit of a headache for developers with its inferior rendering engine, & general wokiness with non-webkit specified CSS. It goes through a new version with each release of macOS, & a few indepentent ones addressing security, support, & speed. They add a 'Developer' menu item with some solid dev tools catering to you & me. These are especially helpful in previewing & troubleshooting Safari specific issues that only render when viewed within Safari itself.

    • Edge

      Edge stumbles out of the gate, performing well on a technical level, but completely lacking in features. These are added over the years, boosting usage in general. They also switch to the Blink engine to avoid the same proprietary pitfalls that plagued IE. Bing shall not be mentioned.

    • Firefox

      Firefox experiences growing pains as it tries to keep up with Chrome. It launches project Quantum to improve its Gecko engine, ultimately rendering existing add-ons for the browser incompatible with newer versions. Firefox usage continues to decline over the years to come.

    • Samsung Internet

      Samsung Internet is the only strictly mobile browser that's managed to secure a solid market share thanks to the success of Samsung smartphones. It's a Chromium browser, like most modern ones, & doesn't do anything espeically remarkable. Well played, Samsung.

    • Opera

      Opera does some interesting things during this time. They add built-in messenger support for Facebook, WhatsApp, Discord, & more, as well as new privacy/VPN functions, & a crypto wallet. They somehow find an increased market share, & even release two additional browsers called Opera GX, geared towards gamers & tech nerds with high customization, resource configurations, & a game community platform, & Opera Crypto Developer, catering heavy-handedly to the crypto community.

    • Brave

      Brave browser is released with built-in ad-blocking despite being largely supported by ad revenue, & big plans for user privacy. It adds features over the years such as a built-in VPN, crypto wallet, its own search engine, a Zoom/Jitsi alternative, increased ad-block functions, & even its own crypto currency (BAT) it uses to reward users opting into their ad experience. Most features have free & paid options.

    • Vivaldi

      Vivaldi browser is released after existing as a virtual community filling a hole left by the demise of My Opera (essentially a social community for Opera lovers). Vivaldi retains the Presto-era features of the Opera of years-past via propiretary modifications despite also running on Chromium.
